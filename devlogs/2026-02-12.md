### State at Session Start

Last session (Feb 10) killed the random noise approach. Core finding: prediction consistency under Gaussian noise measures softmax saturation, not representation quality. At sigma=0.25, CLS cosine similarity dropped to 0.78 but prediction consistency was still 94.3% — because all the flips were borderline examples near 50% confidence.

New direction: measure output space consistency $d_\mathcal{Y}(f(x), f(Tx))$ directly, where $T$ is a real semantic transformation. Hypothesis today: output-space comparison across paraphrases is a principled robustness measure that avoids the random-noise problem.

Still using BERT-base-uncased on SST-2.

### Work Completed

**Built an output-only consistency metric over paraphrase pairs.**

Used a subset of the PAWS dataset (paraphrase adversaries from word scrambling) — took 200 positive pairs from PAWS-QQP where label=1 (confirmed paraphrases). For each pair $(x, Tx)$:

```python
def output_consistency(
    model: nn.Module,
    tokenizer,
    text_a: str,
    text_b: str,
) -> dict[str, float]:
    enc_a = tokenizer(text_a, return_tensors="pt", truncation=True, max_length=128)
    enc_b = tokenizer(text_b, return_tensors="pt", truncation=True, max_length=128)
    with torch.no_grad():
        out_a = model(**enc_a)
        out_b = model(**enc_b)
    prob_a = out_a.logits.softmax(dim=-1)
    prob_b = out_b.logits.softmax(dim=-1)
    return {
        "kl_divergence": kl_div(prob_a, prob_b).item(),
        "l1_distance": (prob_a - prob_b).abs().sum().item(),
        "same_prediction": (prob_a.argmax() == prob_b.argmax()).item(),
    }
```

Also built a simple synonym substitution paraphraser (NLTK WordNet, random synset selection) as a programmatic $T$.

Numbers look good. BERT is consistent across paraphrases. RoBERTa even more so.

**The problem I ran into:** I found 8 training examples in PAWS-QQP that were also in SST-2's training split. For those 8 examples, both $x$ and $Tx$ were in BERT's training data. Prediction consistency on those 8: 1.0. For the remaining 192: 0.958.

That's a small effect here, but it pointed to the fundamental problem.

**Counterexample constructed manually:**

Took SST-2 training example: *"This movie is absolutely brilliant."* (label: positive, BERT confidence: 0.987)

Constructed a paraphrase not in training data: *"This film is profoundly exceptional."* (BERT confidence: 0.981, same prediction)

Then tried: *"This movie is absolutely brilliant but in a way that only people who don't understand cinema would appreciate."* — semantically different, but BERT says positive with confidence 0.891. The model doesn't really understand the negation scope. But it's consistent on the paraphrase of the first sentence.

The issue: a model that has memorized associations between tokens and labels will be consistent on paraphrases of training examples. It will fail on out-of-distribution paraphrases that require compositional understanding. The output-space metric at evaluation time can't distinguish these cases.

### Lab Notes

The output-only metric is measuring something real, but it's measuring the wrong thing for my purposes. It conflates two very different sources of "stability":

1. **Genuine semantic invariance** — the model has learned that "canine" and "dog" refer to the same concept and handles them the same way regardless of context
2. **Superficial memorization** — the model has seen enough sentiment data that these particular word-class associations are locked in, so similar words produce similar outputs on surface grounds

The metric gives the same number for both cases. I want to distinguish them.

The key failure mode I care about: a model that generalizes well on in-distribution paraphrases but collapses on novel paraphrases it hasn't seen during training. The output-only metric never sees this because at evaluation time, the model gives consistent output even for training-distribution examples it's memorized.

Cosine similarity between CLS representations might be better — it's measuring something about the internal state, not just the final output. But I tried this too and it has the same memorization problem in a different form.

I wrote in my notes: *"output collapse hides internal incoherence. need to look inside the model, not just at what it says."*

This is pushing me toward measuring sensitivity at the representation level — how much does the internal state change under semantic-preserving perturbations?

### Hypotheses

1. **Memorized associations produce output stability without representation stability.** A model can return the same prediction for $x$ and $Tx$ while the internal representations $\phi(x)$ and $\phi(Tx)$ are substantially different. If the decision is made by a simple linear head that's overfit on token-output associations, output consistency says nothing about whether representations are semantically organized.

2. **The interesting signal is in representation space, not output space.** The Jacobian $J_f(x)$ at a point $x$ tells you how the output changes for infinitesimal input perturbations — across all directions simultaneously. Restricting to semantic directions in $A_x$ gives the metric I actually want.

3. **Sensitivity needs normalization by perturbation cost.** If I just measure $d_\mathcal{Y}(f(x), f(Tx))$, a large output change under a large transformation is expected and uninformative. The ratio $d_\mathcal{Y}(f(x), f(Tx)) / c(T, x)$ normalizes for the "size" of the transformation and gives something closer to a per-unit-perturbation sensitivity.

### Next Steps

- Abandon output-space consistency as the primary metric
- Formalize a sensitivity functional in representation space: something like $d_\mathcal{Y}(f(x), f(Tx)) / c(T, x)$ but grounded in the theory
- Define the constrained transformation family $\mathcal{T}$ precisely — this is the critical missing piece
- Read Jacovi & Goldberg 2020 (faithfulness vs plausibility in interpretability) and the probing literature — they face the same problem from a different angle
