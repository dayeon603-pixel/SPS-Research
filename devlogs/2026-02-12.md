## [09:00 UTC] Reading Session — Jacovi & Goldberg, Probing Literature

### State at Session Start

Last session (Feb 10) killed the random noise approach and ended with a whiteboard session on the definition of semantic preservation. Decision: operational definition via label preservation. Now reading more carefully to understand where the field's probing metrics break down, so I know exactly what I'm building against.

### Work Completed

**Read:** Jacovi & Goldberg 2020 (second read, more careful). Hewitt & Liang 2019 (probing with selectivity). Belinkov & Glass 2019 (survey of analysis methods in NLP).

Key insight from Hewitt & Liang: probing accuracy conflates two things — what information is present in the representation, and how easily it can be extracted. A simple linear probe can achieve high accuracy on a representation that doesn't actually encode the linguistic structure, if the task is learnable from superficial statistics. They propose "selectivity" (probe accuracy minus control task accuracy) to address this.

This applies directly to my problem. Output-space consistency metrics conflate:
1. The representation genuinely encoding semantic equivalence
2. The linear head having learned token-class associations that happen to generalize to synonyms

Hewitt & Liang's selectivity fix doesn't directly transfer, but the diagnosis is the same.

**Reading Ravichander et al. 2020 (probing benchmarks):** They find that probing results are highly sensitive to the exact probe architecture. Different probes give wildly different conclusions about what information is "in" a representation. This makes me more skeptical of any metric that involves learning a second model on top of the one being tested.

Implication for SPS: the metric should not involve any learned component. It should be a direct measurement of $f_\theta$'s response to structured input variation — no auxiliary probe.

### Results / Observations

Reading session output: three key desiderata for the framework I'm building:
1. No learned probe — direct measurement of $f_\theta$'s Jacobian or sensitivity
2. No output-space-only measurement — must capture representation-level behavior
3. Transformation family $\mathcal{T}$ must be explicit and auditable, not implicit in a learned model

### Hypotheses

1. Any metric that involves training an auxiliary model inherits that model's inductive biases. This makes it impossible to separate "what BERT knows" from "what the probe knows."
2. A direct Jacobian-based measurement avoids the probe problem entirely — no auxiliary model, no circularity.

### Next Steps

- Implement output-space consistency metric as a baseline (even though I already know it's insufficient — I need empirical confirmation)
- Start sketching the Jacobian-based alternative

## [11:05 UTC] Implementation Session — Output-Only Consistency Metric

### State at Session Start

Reading sessions done. Now building the output-only consistency metric — not because I think it will work, but to have an empirical comparison point and to explicitly document what it misses.

Prior failure noted: Gaussian noise experiment showed that output stability under noise measures softmax saturation, not representation quality. Hypothesis today: output-space consistency over paraphrase pairs has the same problem but in disguise.

Still using BERT-base-uncased on SST-2.

### Work Completed

**Built an output-only consistency metric over paraphrase pairs.**

Used PAWS dataset (paraphrase adversaries from word scrambling) — 200 positive pairs from PAWS-QQP where label=1 (confirmed paraphrases). For each pair $(x, Tx)$:

```python
def output_consistency(
    model: nn.Module,
    tokenizer: PreTrainedTokenizer,
    text_a: str,
    text_b: str,
) -> dict[str, float]:
    enc_a = tokenizer(text_a, return_tensors="pt", truncation=True, max_length=128)
    enc_b = tokenizer(text_b, return_tensors="pt", truncation=True, max_length=128)
    with torch.no_grad():
        out_a = model(**enc_a)
        out_b = model(**enc_b)
    prob_a = out_a.logits.softmax(dim=-1)
    prob_b = out_b.logits.softmax(dim=-1)
    return {
        "kl_divergence": F.kl_div(prob_a.log(), prob_b, reduction="sum").item(),
        "l1_distance": (prob_a - prob_b).abs().sum().item(),
        "same_prediction": (prob_a.argmax() == prob_b.argmax()).item(),
    }
```

Also built a programmatic synonym substitution paraphraser (NLTK WordNet, random synset selection, controlled substitution rate).

### Results / Observations

**PAWS positive pairs (confirmed paraphrases, n=200):**

| Metric | BERT-base | RoBERTa-base |
|---|---|---|
| Same prediction rate | 0.961 | 0.974 |
| Mean KL divergence | 0.031 | 0.019 |
| Mean L1 distance | 0.048 | 0.033 |

**Programmatic synonym substitution (n=200, BERT-base only):**

| Substitution rate | Same prediction | Mean KL |
|---|---|---|
| 10% tokens swapped | 0.982 | 0.014 |
| 25% tokens swapped | 0.951 | 0.038 |
| 40% tokens swapped | 0.917 | 0.062 |

Numbers look good. BERT appears highly consistent across paraphrases.

**The memorization problem I found:** 8 training examples from PAWS-QQP appeared in SST-2's training split. For those 8, both $x$ and $Tx$ were in BERT's training data. Prediction consistency on those 8: 1.000. For the remaining 192: 0.958. Small effect here — but it illustrated the structural problem.

**Counter-example constructed manually:**

- Training example: *"This movie is absolutely brilliant."* (BERT confidence: 0.987 positive)
- Paraphrase: *"This film is profoundly exceptional."* (BERT confidence: 0.981 positive, same prediction)
- Novel negation scope: *"This movie is absolutely brilliant but in a way that only people who don't understand cinema would appreciate."* — BERT says positive (0.891). Doesn't understand the semantics.

BERT is consistent on the paraphrase of the first sentence (that part works), but it's consistent because the tokens "absolutely brilliant" are strongly associated with positive sentiment in training data — not because the model understands the semantic relationship. The output-only metric can't distinguish these cases.

### Lab Notes

The output-only metric is measuring something real, but it conflates two sources of "stability":

1. **Genuine semantic invariance** — the model has learned that "canine" and "dog" refer to the same concept, generalizes to novel paraphrases
2. **Superficial memorization** — the model has seen enough sentiment data that specific token-class associations are locked in; similar surface forms produce similar outputs

The metric gives the same number for both. I need to distinguish them.

Also noticed: cosine similarity between CLS representations for paraphrase pairs:

| Paraphrase type | Mean CLS cosine sim | Mean output KL |
|---|---|---|
| PAWS positive pairs | 0.823 | 0.031 |
| Random negative pairs | 0.441 | 0.187 |
| Synonym substitution 25% | 0.871 | 0.038 |

The CLS embeddings are meaningfully different ($\cos = 0.82$) even for confirmed paraphrases. The model is producing the same output via different internal paths. That's precisely the "right for the wrong reasons" phenomenon from Jacovi & Goldberg.

Wrote in notes: *"output collapse hides internal incoherence. need to look inside the model, not just at what it says."*

### Hypotheses

1. **Memorized associations produce output stability without representation stability.** A model can return the same prediction for $x$ and $Tx$ while $\phi(x)$ and $\phi(Tx)$ are geometrically far apart. If the decision head is overfit to token-class associations, output consistency says nothing about representation structure.

2. **The interesting signal is in representation space, not output space.** The Jacobian $J_f(x)$ tells you how the output changes for infinitesimal input perturbations across all directions. Restricting to semantic directions in $A_x$ gives the metric I actually want.

3. **Sensitivity needs normalization by perturbation cost.** The ratio $d_\mathcal{Y}(f(x), f(Tx)) / c(T, x)$ normalizes for perturbation size and gives something closer to a per-unit-perturbation sensitivity — interpretable as a local Lipschitz constant.

### Next Steps

- Abandon output-space consistency as the primary metric
- Formalize a sensitivity functional in representation space
- Define the constrained transformation family $\mathcal{T}$ precisely — this is the critical missing piece

## [18:45 UTC] Whiteboard Session — Formalizing What I Want to Measure

### State at Session Start

Two experiments done, both failed for clearly understood reasons. I now have a concrete picture of what's wrong with existing approaches. This session: attempt to write down the formal definition of the metric I actually want.

### Work Completed

No code. Pure math session.

**Goal:** a metric $M(f_\theta, \mathcal{T})$ that satisfies:
- $M = 1$ if and only if $f_\theta$ is perfectly invariant to $\mathcal{T}$
- $M > 0$ always (lower-bounded away from 0 for sensible $f_\theta$)
- $M$ is not measuring softmax saturation
- $M$ is not measuring memorization
- $M$ can be computed from the model itself without an auxiliary probe

**First attempt:**

$$M_1(f_\theta, \mathcal{T}) = \mathbb{E}_{x, T}\left[\cos(\phi_\theta(x), \phi_\theta(Tx))\right]$$

Problem: representation-level, avoids the output-collapse problem. But: what's the baseline? A model that maps everything to the zero vector scores 1.0 trivially.

**Second attempt:** normalize by an arbitrary baseline

$$M_2(f_\theta, \mathcal{T}) = \frac{\mathbb{E}_{x,T}[\cos(\phi(x), \phi(Tx))]}{\mathbb{E}_{x,T'}[\cos(\phi(x), \phi(T'x))]}$$

where $T'$ is an arbitrary (semantically unconstrained) transformation. Problem: what's the reference distribution for $T'$? Arbitrary in what space?

**Third attempt:** ratio of output changes, normalized by perturbation magnitude

$$M_3(f_\theta, x, T) = \frac{d_\mathcal{Y}(f_\theta(x), f_\theta(Tx))}{c(T, x)}$$

Take supremum over $\mathcal{T}$ and expectation over data. Map to $(0, 1]$ via exp(-·).

This feels right. The sup captures worst-case behavior over admissible transformations. The normalization by $c(T, x)$ makes it scale-invariant. The exp maps the non-negative real line to $(0, 1]$ with 1 = perfect invariance.

$$\mathrm{SPS}_\varepsilon(f_\theta) := \exp\left(-\mathbb{E}_{x \sim \mathcal{D}}\left[\sup_{\substack{T \in \mathcal{T} \\ 0 < c(T,x) \leq \varepsilon}} \frac{d_\mathcal{Y}(f_\theta(x), f_\theta(Tx))}{c(T,x)}\right]\right)$$

Why exp(-·) specifically? Three reasons:
1. Maps $[0, \infty) \to (0, 1]$ — interpretable
2. $\mathrm{SPS} = 1$ iff $\mathbb{E}[\mathrm{Sens}] = 0$ iff perfect invariance
3. Intuition: this is like the moment generating function of the sensitivity distribution evaluated at $-1$; there might be a clean information-theoretic characterization

Need to verify: is this well-defined? Specifically, does the supremum inside the expectation need to be finite? — Yes, this requires integrability (A4). Write it as an assumption.

### Results / Observations

Drafted Definition 1 (sensitivity functional) and Definition 2 (SPS). These match what ended up in the final paper. The choice of exp(-·) was made here.

Key unresolved question from this session: what ensures the sup is finite? Compactness of $A_x$ (A2) plus local Lipschitz continuity of $f_\theta$ would do it, but I haven't verified that the Jacobian characterization follows. That's the next major theoretical task.

### Hypotheses

1. The Jacobian connection should fall out from the $\varepsilon \to 0$ limit of the sensitivity functional — this is Theorem 2 in the eventual paper.
2. The compactness assumption (A2) on $A_x$ is what makes the sup in the sensitivity functional finite and the Jacobian characterization tight.
3. The exp(-·) choice may have an information-theoretic interpretation I haven't found yet.

- Derive the Jacobian connection in the $\varepsilon \to 0$ limit — this is Theorem 2
