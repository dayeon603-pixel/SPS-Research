Intended statement (first draft):

> **Theorem 4 (Sequential Transformation Stability).** Under A5.3 (closure under composition), for $T_1, T_2 \in \mathcal{T}$ with $c(T_1, x) \leq \varepsilon$:
>
> $$d_\mathcal{Y}(f(x), f(T_2 T_1 x)) \leq \mathrm{Sens}_{\mathcal{T},\varepsilon}(f; x) \cdot c(T_1, x) + \mathrm{Sens}_{\mathcal{T},\varepsilon}(f; T_1 x) \cdot c(T_2, T_1 x)$$

Proof attempt:

Triangle inequality in $(\mathcal{Y}, d_\mathcal{Y})$:

$$d_\mathcal{Y}(f(x), f(T_2 T_1 x)) \leq d_\mathcal{Y}(f(x), f(T_1 x)) + d_\mathcal{Y}(f(T_1 x), f(T_2 T_1 x))$$

First term: Since $T_1 \in \mathcal{T}$ and $c(T_1, x) \leq \varepsilon$:

$$d_\mathcal{Y}(f(x), f(T_1 x)) \leq \mathrm{Sens}_{\mathcal{T},\varepsilon}(f; x) \cdot c(T_1, x)$$

Fine.

Second term: The plan is to bound $d_\mathcal{Y}(f(T_1 x), f(T_2 T_1 x))$ using sensitivity at basepoint $T_1 x$. Need: $T_2$ is in $\mathcal{T}$, and $c(T_2, T_1 x) \leq \varepsilon$.

The problem: I stated the hypothesis as "$c(T_1, x) \leq \varepsilon$" but said nothing about $c(T_2, T_1 x)$. The hypothesis only constrains the first transformation, not the second.

Without $c(T_2, T_1 x) \leq \varepsilon$, the second term uses $\mathrm{Sens}_{\mathcal{T},\varepsilon}(f; T_1 x)$ but $T_2$ applied at basepoint $T_1 x$ might push outside the $\varepsilon$-ball. The sensitivity functional $\mathrm{Sens}_{\mathcal{T},\varepsilon}(f; T_1 x)$ is only defined for transformations with cost $\leq \varepsilon$. If $c(T_2, T_1 x) > \varepsilon$, the bound in the second term doesn't apply.

**The fix: add $c(T_2, T_1 x) \leq \varepsilon$ as an explicit hypothesis.**

Revised theorem adds the constraint: both $c(T_1, x) \leq \varepsilon$ AND $c(T_2, T_1 x) \leq \varepsilon$ must hold. The second transformation's cost is measured at the already-transformed basepoint $T_1 x$.

This is a non-trivial requirement. A5.3 (closure under composition) only guarantees that $T_2 \circ T_1 \in \mathcal{T}$ â€” it says nothing about whether $c(T_2, T_1 x) \leq \varepsilon$. In the paper I note that A5 provides a sufficient condition when $c(T_1, x) \leq \varepsilon/2$ and $c(T_2, T_1 x) \leq \varepsilon/2$ â€” the composition stays within $\varepsilon$ by the triangle inequality for $c$. But the theorem as stated requires the two-sided constraint explicitly.

Added to A5 in `theory/definitions.md`:

> The constraint $c(T_2, T_1 x) \leq \varepsilon$ is not automatic from $c(T_1, x) \leq \varepsilon$ alone. It must be imposed as a separate hypothesis in Theorem 4. A sufficient condition is $c(T_1, x) \leq \varepsilon/2$ and $c(T_2, T_1 x) \leq \varepsilon/2$.

**Committed as `fix(theory): theorem-4 add chain constraint c(T2, T1x) <= eps`.**

**Why this matters geometrically:**

Think of $\varepsilon$ as defining a neighborhood around $x$. After applying $T_1$, we're at $T_1 x$ â€” which may or may not be close to $x$. If $T_1$ is a large transformation (but still $\leq \varepsilon$), then $T_1 x$ might be far from $x$. The second transformation $T_2$ is now applied at $T_1 x$. The sensitivity at $T_1 x$ is $\mathrm{Sens}_{\mathcal{T},\varepsilon}(f; T_1 x)$ â€” but this only covers perturbations of size $\leq \varepsilon$ from $T_1 x$. If $T_2$ is large when applied at $T_1 x$, it leaves the sensitivity's domain.

The chain constraint $c(T_2, T_1 x) \leq \varepsilon$ says: the second step must also be small, measured at the intermediate point.

### Lab Notes

Two proof errors in two weeks (Theorem 2 on Mar 1, Theorem 4 today). Both were missing-constraint errors: asserting a result without fully specifying the domain where it holds. This is a pattern I need to watch for.

The pattern is: I write the conclusion I want, and only then try to prove it. When the proof requires a constraint that isn't in the hypothesis, I should either add the constraint or weaken the conclusion. In both cases I added the constraint. In Theorem 2 the constraint was compactness (A2). In Theorem 4 it was the chain constraint.

The chain constraint is geometrically natural once you see it. Sequential transformations each need to stay within the sensitivity radius â€” otherwise you're composing something local (sensitivity at $x$ within $\varepsilon$) with something that leaves the local neighborhood. The bound is only local.

I think there's also a more general principle here: for any result that involves evaluating a function at a transformed input (like "sensitivity at $T_1 x$"), you need to ensure the transformed input is in the domain where the function is defined. This is a domain-tracking requirement that functional analysis proofs are full of but that's easy to skip informally.

### Hypotheses

1. **Theorem 4's chain constraint is automatically satisfied for $\varepsilon \leq 0.1$ and synonym/embedding perturbation families.** The maximum transformation magnitude for a synonym substitution with substitution_prob=0.15 on 128-token sequences is approximately $\|v_{\text{syn}} - v_{\text{orig}}\|_F / \sqrt{seq \times hidden} \approx 0.03â€“0.08$ in practice. 2. **The composition bound in Theorem 4 is loose by a factor proportional to the spectral gap.** The triangle inequality step is tight only when the path $x \to T_1 x \to T_2 T_1 x$ is adversarial. For models with high $\bar{\gamma}$, the semantic directions contribute less to sensitivity, so the bound is looser. A tighter bound using the spectral gap directly would be possible but complex.

3. **Theorem 4 is most relevant for multi-hop paraphrase chains.** Back-translation through multiple pivot languages can be modeled as a composition of transformations $T_{\text{en} \to \text{de}} \circ T_{\text{de} \to \text{en}}$. The chain constraint ensures both hops stay within the admissible radius. This is a practical use case for the theorem.
