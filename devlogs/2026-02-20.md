## [09:00 UTC] Whiteboard Session — Axioms for T and Formal Assumptions

### State at Session Start

Prototype estimator working (Feb 15 evening). Definitions 1 and 2 drafted informally. Today starts with a theory session before touching code: I need to nail down what axioms the transformation family $\mathcal{T}$ needs to satisfy for the proofs to go through.

### Work Completed

No code. One hour formalizing the axiom list.

**What does $\mathcal{T}$ actually need?**

Working backwards from the theorems I want to prove:

- **Proposition 1** ($0 < \mathrm{SPS} \leq 1$): needs $\mathrm{Sens} \geq 0$ (guaranteed by $d_\mathcal{Y} \geq 0$ and $c > 0$) and $\mathbb{E}[\mathrm{Sens}] < \infty$ (integrability). I need an explicit assumption for the latter. → **A4 (Integrability).**

- **Theorem 2** (Jacobian connection): needs $f_\theta$ Fréchet differentiable (→ **A1**) and the set $A_x$ compact so I can interchange sup and limit (→ **A2 (Transformation Compactness)**).

- **SPS well-defined**: needs the sensitivity map $x \mapsto \mathrm{Sens}_{\mathcal{T},\varepsilon}(f_\theta; x)$ to be measurable with respect to $\mathcal{D}$. → **A3 (Measurability).**

- **Theorem 4** (sequential composition): needs $\mathcal{T}$ closed under composition. → **A5 (Transformation Family Axioms)**, specifically the closure part.

**Axiom A5 in detail:**

The transformation family $\mathcal{T}$ must satisfy:
1. **Identity:** $\mathrm{Id} \in \mathcal{T}$, with $c(\mathrm{Id}, x) = 0$. (The trivial transformation is in the family.)
2. **Semantic preservation:** Every $T \in \mathcal{T}$ preserves the ground-truth label at every $x \in \mathrm{supp}(\mathcal{D})$.
3. **Closure under composition:** If $T_1, T_2 \in \mathcal{T}$, then $T_2 \circ T_1 \in \mathcal{T}$. (Required for Theorem 4.)

The identity condition is important: it means $c(T, x) = 0$ is achievable, so the sup in Definition 1 is over a non-empty set. (If the only transformations in $\mathcal{T}$ have $c(T,x) > \varepsilon$, the sensitivity is zero vacuously — not interesting.)

**Question I couldn't resolve today:** Does A5.3 (closure under composition) hold for practical families like WordNet synonym substitution? If I substitute "dog" → "canine" and then "canine" → "hound", is the composition guaranteed to be in the family? Not necessarily — WordNet doesn't guarantee that the composition of two synonym substitutions gives another synonym substitution. Need to either relax A5.3 or restrict the theorem's scope.

For now, leaving A5.3 as stated and noting that Theorem 4 applies only when the family satisfies closure. The synonym family satisfies it approximately.

**Drafted all five assumptions** — A1 through A5 — in draft form. These will go into `theory/definitions.md`.

### Results / Observations

Axiom list A1–A5 drafted. Key structural insight: A4 (integrability) is the assumption that prevents $\mathrm{SPS} = 0$. Without it, Proposition 1 only gives $\mathrm{SPS} \geq 0$. The strict lower bound requires the expectation to be finite.

### Hypotheses

1. A5.3 (composition closure) may need to be weakened to "$T_2 \circ T_1 \in \mathcal{T}$ when $c(T_1, x) + c(T_2, T_1 x) \leq \varepsilon$" to handle practical families correctly.
2. A4 is the most fragile assumption — integrability fails if the model has unbounded sensitivity somewhere in the support of $\mathcal{D}$. For finite-parameter neural nets this shouldn't happen, but worth checking.

## [13:31 UTC] Implementation Session — Sensitivity Estimator + First SPS Numbers

### State at Session Start

Axioms drafted. Definitions 1 and 2 stable. Now: write the actual Python implementation of the sensitivity estimator, get it running, get numbers.

Setup: `bert-base-uncased` (110M params, 12 layers, $d = 768$), SST-2 dev split, CPU.

### Work Completed

**Drafted `StructuredSensitivityEstimator` prototype:**

```python
def estimate_sensitivity(
    model: nn.Module,
    embeddings: torch.Tensor,      # (B, seq, hidden)
    transform_family: TransformationFamily,
    divergence_fn: DivergenceFn,
    epsilon: float,
    m: int,
) -> torch.Tensor:                  # (B,) — pointwise sensitivity
    fx = model(inputs_embeds=embeddings).last_hidden_state[:, 0, :]
    max_ratios = torch.zeros(embeddings.size(0))
    for _ in range(m):
        tx, cx = transform_family.sample(embeddings, epsilon)
        ftx = model(inputs_embeds=tx).last_hidden_state[:, 0, :]
        div = divergence_fn(fx, ftx)
        ratio = div / cx.clamp(min=1e-12)
        max_ratios = torch.maximum(max_ratios, ratio)
    return max_ratios
```

This is the skeleton of `StructuredSensitivityEstimator.estimate()` in `src/sps/core.py`. Not production-ready — no Pydantic config, no logging — but the logic matches Definition 1 exactly.

**Design decisions:**

1. **CLS token as representation.** $f_\theta(x) = $ [CLS] hidden state at final layer. This is what the task head is applied to.

2. **Cosine divergence as $d_\mathcal{Y}$.** $d_\mathcal{Y}(a, b) = 1 - \cos(a, b)$, bounded in $[0, 2]$, invariant to representation magnitude. Will keep L2 as an alternative option.

3. **Max over $m$ instead of mean.** The max approximates the sup in Definition 1. Using mean gives a different (weaker) quantity — average sensitivity rather than worst-case.

**Ran first quantitative SPS estimate** on BERT-base, SST-2 dev split (n=100 examples, m=8 transformations, synonym substitution family, $\varepsilon = 0.1$):

| Metric | Value |
|---|---|
| Mean pointwise sensitivity | 0.341 |
| Std pointwise sensitivity | 0.187 |
| Empirical SPS | $\exp(-0.341) = 0.711$ |
| Min sensitivity | 0.018 |
| Max sensitivity | 0.912 |

**Sanity check** — random BERT-base (untrained, same architecture):

| Metric | Trained BERT | Random BERT |
|---|---|---|
| Mean sensitivity | 0.341 | 1.847 |
| Empirical SPS | 0.711 | 0.158 |

Trained model has substantially higher SPS. Expected — a model that has learned structured representations should be more stable to semantic-preserving perturbations.

### Results / Observations

First SPS numbers: BERT-base = 0.711, random = 0.158. The gap is large and in the right direction.

Sensitivity distribution is right-skewed: most examples cluster in [0.1, 0.4], a tail of hard examples reaches 0.7–0.9. The mean doesn't fully summarize this — should also report the full distribution eventually.

**Note on the estimator:** With $m=8$ transformations per example, the max-over-m may not be tight for all inputs. For sequences with many synonymizable positions ($K \gg 8$), the empirical sup underestimates the true sup. Will need to check sensitivity of SPS to $m$ — hypothesize $m=32$ is sufficient for reliable estimates.

### Hypotheses

1. **$m=32$ per input is sufficient for reliable SPS estimation.** The number of synonymizable positions per 128-token sequence is roughly 15–20. With $m=32$, most configurations are covered. Should verify by checking SPS stability as $m$ increases from 8 to 128.

2. **Sensitivity distribution is bimodal: low-sensitivity cluster (clean examples) and high-sensitivity cluster (hard/borderline examples).** The max-based estimator will be dominated by the high-sensitivity cluster.

3. **The gap between trained and random SPS will persist across random seeds.** The gap (0.711 vs 0.158) is large enough to be seed-independent.

## [19:45 UTC] Whiteboard Session — Why the Exponential Form?

### State at Session Start

First numbers in hand. Quick whiteboard session on a theoretical question I've been postponing: why exactly does the exponential form $\exp(-\mathbb{E}[\mathrm{Sens}])$ make sense? I've been defending it on practical grounds (maps to $(0,1]$, SPS=1 iff perfect invariance) but I want a deeper justification.

### Work Completed

**The moment-generating function connection:**

$\mathrm{SPS} = \exp(-\mathbb{E}[\mathrm{Sens}])$ can be written as $M_{-\mathrm{Sens}}(1)$ where $M_X(t) = \mathbb{E}[e^{tX}]$ is the moment generating function of the random variable $X = -\mathrm{Sens}$.

The log of this is $\log(\mathrm{SPS}) = -\mathbb{E}[\mathrm{Sens}] = K_{-\mathrm{Sens}}(1)$ where $K_X(t) = \log M_X(t)$ is the cumulant generating function evaluated at $t=1$. But actually, $K_X(1) = \log \mathbb{E}[e^X]$, not $\mathbb{E}[X]$ in general. These are equal only when $X$ is deterministic or we're working at $t \to 0$.

Wait — I'm confusing myself. $\log \mathrm{SPS} = -\mathbb{E}[\mathrm{Sens}]$ is exactly the negative mean, not the log of the MGF. So SPS is $e^{-\mu}$ where $\mu = \mathbb{E}[\mathrm{Sens}]$. This is simpler than I was making it — it's just $e^{-\mu}$, not an MGF.

Why $e^{-\mu}$ and not, say, $1/(1+\mu)$ or $\tanh(\mu)$? Three reasons I can defend:

1. **Analytical tractability.** The derivative $\frac{d}{d\mu} e^{-\mu} = -e^{-\mu}$ is smooth everywhere. The function $1/(1+\mu)$ has a pole at $\mu = -1$ (not relevant here since $\mu \geq 0$, but still).

2. **Multiplicative structure.** For two independent models $f_1, f_2$ composed in series (Theorem 4), if the sensitivity of the composition is additive ($\mathrm{Sens}_{f_2 \circ f_1} \approx \mathrm{Sens}_{f_1} + \mathrm{Sens}_{f_2}$), then $\mathrm{SPS}_{f_2 \circ f_1} = e^{-(\mu_1 + \mu_2)} = \mathrm{SPS}_{f_1} \cdot \mathrm{SPS}_{f_2}$. The exponential form makes composed SPS multiplicative — a natural structural property.

3. **Connection to stability theory.** In dynamical systems, $e^{-\lambda t}$ measures how quickly perturbations decay under a flow with Lyapunov exponent $\lambda$. The SPS formula has the same form with $\mu$ as the effective "Lyapunov exponent" of the semantic perturbation family. This is suggestive of a dynamical interpretation I haven't fully developed.

The information-theoretic interpretation I was fishing for earlier is probably this: $\log(1/\mathrm{SPS}) = \mathbb{E}[\mathrm{Sens}]$ is the expected log-sensitivity, which looks like a cross-entropy term. But I can't make this precise without a probability model over sensitivity values.

### Results / Observations

No new numbers. Theoretical justification for exponential form: multiplicative structure under composition is the strongest argument. The Lyapunov exponent analogy is suggestive but not rigorous.

### Hypotheses

1. SPS of composed models is multiplicative: $\mathrm{SPS}(f_2 \circ f_1) \approx \mathrm{SPS}(f_1) \cdot \mathrm{SPS}(f_2)$ when the composition's sensitivity is approximately additive. This needs verification empirically (layer-wise SPS products should approximate full-model SPS).
