### Work Completed

**Theoretical framing session — no new code.**

The core realization, written in notes at 10:23:

*"not all perturbations are equal — only structure-preserving ones should count"*

This sounds obvious but it took two weeks of failed experiments to arrive at. The precise claim: measuring stability under a perturbation is only meaningful if the perturbation has a known semantic relationship to the original input. Random noise has no semantic relationship. Output-space comparison has the semantic relationship but collapses the internal structure.

The needed construct: a **constrained transformation family** $\mathcal{T}$ — a set of operators $T : \mathcal{X} \to \mathcal{X}$ such that:
- $Tx$ and $x$ have the same task-relevant label (semantic preservation)
- $c(T, x) = \|Tx - x\|$ measures the "size" of the transformation
- The family is parameterized by $\varepsilon$: only transformations with $c(T, x) \leq \varepsilon$ are admissible at radius $\varepsilon$

The key question immediately raised: **how do you define "semantic preservation" without circularity?**

Three options I considered:

**Option 1: Axiomatic definition.** $T$ is semantic-preserving if there exists a ground-truth label function $\ell : \mathcal{X} \to \mathcal{Y}$ such that $\ell(Tx) = \ell(x)$. Clean and formal. Problem: we don't have access to $\ell$ — it's the thing we're trying to approximate with $f_\theta$.

**Option 2: Operational definition via curated families.** Build $\mathcal{T}$ from hand-curated families with known semantic properties:
- $\mathcal{T}_{\text{syn}}$: synonym substitution (WordNet)
- $\mathcal{T}_{\text{para}}$: controlled paraphrase
- $\mathcal{T}_{\text{back}}$: back-translation (round-trip through another language)

Semantic preservation is inherited from the construction of the family. Less elegant but tractable. Human annotation is too expensive at scale. This is the approach I'm going with.

**Option 3: NLI-based filter.** Use an entailment model to check $\text{entails}(x, Tx) \wedge \text{entails}(Tx, x)$ (bidirectional entailment). Rejects $T$ if it changes the meaning. Problem: circular — evaluating $f_\theta$ using another model's semantic judgments. Also expensive.

Decision: Option 2. The theoretical elegance of Option 1 and the automatic scalability of Option 3 are both less important than being able to actually run experiments.

**Also looked at:**
- AdvGLUE: tests worst-case adversarial inputs targeting accuracy. Different framing — they want to break the model. I want to characterize it neutrally.
- CheckList: tests behavioral expectations (MFT, INV, DIR tests). Closest to what I'm doing but still accuracy-focused and template-based rather than continuous.
- Structural probing (Hewitt & Manning 2019): probing for linguistic structure in representations. Adjacent — they're asking whether specific structures are present. I'm asking whether representations are invariant to surface-form variation.

None of these are measuring what I want to measure.

### Results / Observations

No numerical results today. Theoretical session.

Key conceptual outputs:
- The constrained transformation family $\mathcal{T}$ is the foundational construct
- Semantic preservation via operational definition (curated families)
- The sensitivity functional will be $\sup_{T \in \mathcal{T}, c(T,x) \leq \varepsilon} d_\mathcal{Y}(f(x), f(Tx)) / c(T, x)$
- Aggregation via $\exp(-\mathbb{E}[\text{Sens}])$ maps to $(0, 1]$: interpretable

Why exponential? Three reasons I can defend:
1. Maps $[0, \infty) \to (0, 1]$: interpretable, bounded
2. $\text{SPS} = 1$ iff $\mathbb{E}[\text{Sens}] = 0$ iff model is perfectly invariant: semantically clean
3. Information-theoretic intuition: something like an entropy of the sensitivity distribution. Haven't formalized this but it feels right

### Lab Notes

The thing that keeps nagging: defining "semantic preservation" without an oracle is inherently approximate. WordNet synonyms aren't perfect synonyms. Back-translation changes meaning subtly. The operational definition has noise.

But actually this might be a feature, not a bug. A metric that requires perfect semantic preservation is measuring something that doesn't exist in practice. The SPS framework is measuring stability under transformations that are *approximately* meaning-preserving — which is the practically meaningful question.

AdvGLUE and CheckList were helpful to look at, but their framing is fundamentally different: they're optimizing for adversarial attacks. The adversarial framing biases toward finding edge cases. I want something that characterizes the typical behavior under semantic-preserving variation. That's a different question and there's no existing benchmark for it.

The circularity problem in Option 3 (NLI filter) is actually deeper than I wrote above. If I use a RoBERTa-NLI model to certify that $Tx$ semantically preserves $x$, and then I compute SPS of a different RoBERTa model, I'm using one model's semantic judgments to evaluate another model. Any systematic biases in the NLI model propagate into the SPS estimate. This is correlated error, not independent validation.

### Hypotheses

1. **Operational definition of semantic preservation is sufficient for practical SPS estimates.** As long as the curated families have a low false positive rate (most members genuinely preserve meaning), the SPS estimate will be approximately correct. Systematic false positives (transformations that appear semantic but change meaning) would bias SPS downward — a conservative direction.

2. **The transformation magnitude normalization $c(T, x)$ is essential.** Without it, larger perturbations trivially produce larger output changes. The ratio $d_\mathcal{Y} / c$ is what makes the metric meaningful across transformation scales.

3. **Exponential aggregation has better theoretical properties than linear averaging.** $\exp(-\mathbb{E}[\text{Sens}])$ is the moment-generating function of $-\text{Sens}$ evaluated at $t=1$. There may be a natural connection to concentration inequalities or large deviation bounds here. Worth exploring later.
