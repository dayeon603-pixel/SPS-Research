**Full SPS comparison: BERT vs RoBERTa.**

Loaded both models, computed SPS on SST-2 test split. Also computed standard accuracy and mean cosine similarity between original and perturbed [CLS] representations (as a baseline comparison).

Back-translation via MarianMT required a separate pipeline — loaded `Helsinki-NLP/opus-mt-en-de` and `Helsinki-NLP/opus-mt-de-en` for round-trip translation.

```python
def back_translate(text: str, en_de, de_en, tokenizer_en_de, tokenizer_de_en) -> str:
    tokens = tokenizer_en_de(text, return_tensors="pt", truncation=True, max_length=128)
    with torch.no_grad():
        de = en_de.generate(**tokens)
    de_text = tokenizer_en_de.decode(de[0], skip_special_tokens=True)
    tokens2 = tokenizer_de_en(de_text, return_tensors="pt", truncation=True, max_length=128)
    with torch.no_grad():
        en = de_en.generate(**tokens2)
    return tokenizer_de_en.decode(en[0], skip_special_tokens=True)
```

### Results / Observations

**Main results table (SST-2 test split, n=1,821):**

| Model | Accuracy | SPS (synonym) | SPS (back-trans) | Mean CLS cosine sim |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| bert-base-uncased | 91.2% | 0.717 | 0.584 | 0.881 |
| roberta-base | 94.1% | 0.814 | 0.673 | 0.921 |

**Key findings:**

1. RoBERTa has higher accuracy (94.1% vs 91.2%) and higher SPS on both transformation families.

2. The SPS gap between models is larger than the accuracy gap. Accuracy difference: 2.9 percentage points. SPS (synonym) difference: 0.097. SPS (back-trans) difference: 0.089. SPS is capturing something that accuracy doesn't.

3. Back-translation SPS is systematically lower than synonym SPS for both models (BERT: 0.584 vs 0.717; RoBERTa: 0.673 vs 0.814). This makes sense — back-translation introduces more surface variation (changes word order, introduces paraphrases) while ideally preserving meaning. The model sees larger perturbations.

4. The CLS cosine similarity (0.881 vs 0.921) shows RoBERTa representations are more stable under synonym substitution. But the magnitude of this difference (0.040) is smaller than the SPS difference (0.097). SPS, which also accounts for how the output changes, is more discriminative.

**Sensitivity distribution (synonym SPS):**

| Percentile | BERT-base sensitivity | RoBERTa-base sensitivity |
|---|---|---|
| 10th | 0.041 | 0.029 |
| 25th | 0.112 | 0.082 |
| 50th (median) | 0.283 | 0.191 |
| 75th | 0.482 | 0.351 |
| 90th | 0.721 | 0.529 |
| 99th | 1.247 | 0.891 |

Both distributions are right-skewed. BERT has a heavier tail — more examples with very high sensitivity. The 99th percentile BERT sensitivity (1.247) is substantially higher than RoBERTa's (0.891).

### Lab Notes

The SPS vs accuracy decoupling is the thing I've been waiting to see. 2.9 pp accuracy gap, 9.7 pp SPS gap (synonym). The question I need to answer next: can I find two models with the *same* accuracy but *different* SPS? That's the smoking gun for the paper — it would prove that SPS captures information orthogonal to accuracy.

Candidates: BERT-large vs RoBERTa-base? They're close in accuracy on SST-2 (~93%). Or look at fine-tuned variants with different regularization.

The back-translation result is interesting from a different angle. MarianMT round-trip translation is an imperfect semantic-preserving transformation — it sometimes changes meaning subtly. If the transformation introduces semantic changes, it violates A5.2 (semantic preservation), and the SPS computed under it is not theoretically clean. Need to add NLI verification as a filter for back-translation pairs, even though I argued against NLI-based semantic checking earlier. The argument against was circularity in defining the family — but as a post-hoc filter (not the primary family definition), NLI checking is fine.

The mean CLS cosine similarity metric (0.881 / 0.921) is more interpretable to non-practitioners than SPS (0.717 / 0.814) but is less informative. CLS similarity measures representation change, not output sensitivity weighted by perturbation cost. They're related but measuring different things.

The WordNet memory issue is more serious than I thought. Running at 3.9 GB just for synonym map construction is going to make large-scale experiments infeasible on machines with limited RAM. The vocab cap fix is urgent.

### Hypotheses

1. **After the WordNet fix (vocab_size cap at 10k), SPS estimates will decrease slightly.** The random orthogonal directions that were being used for tokens 12k–30k are not semantically grounded. They're measuring sensitivity along arbitrary directions. Sensitivity under random directions may be lower or higher than under synonym directions depending on the model. Prediction: SPS will decrease slightly (synonym directions are harder than random in expectation) but the BERT vs RoBERTa ordering will be preserved.

2. **There exist model pairs with matching accuracy and meaningfully different SPS.** BERT-large (~93% on SST-2) and RoBERTa-base (~94%) are close in accuracy. If RoBERTa's SPS advantage persists while accuracy converges, this is the evidence needed for the paper.

3. **RoBERTa's higher SPS is caused by its longer pretraining and dynamic masking.** BERT uses static masking — the same tokens are always masked during training. RoBERTa uses dynamic masking — the mask changes every epoch. Dynamic masking forces the model to build more robust representations since any token can be missing. This should reduce sensitivity to synonym substitution (a similar perturbation to masking, geometrically).
