### Work Completed

**Jacobian connection derived.**

In the $\varepsilon \to 0$ limit, consider a smooth transformation family where $T_\alpha^v(x) = x + \alpha v$ for unit direction $v \in A_x$ and $\alpha \in (0, \varepsilon]$. Then:

$$\frac{\|f(x + \alpha v) - f(x)\|}{\alpha} \xrightarrow{\alpha \to 0} \|J_f(x)\, v\|$$

by the definition of the Fréchet derivative ($A1$). So pointwise sensitivity in the $\varepsilon \to 0$ limit is exactly the JVP norm along $v$.

Taking the sup over all $v \in A_x$:

$$\lim_{\varepsilon \to 0} \mathrm{Sens}_{\mathcal{T},\varepsilon}(f; x) = \sup_{v \in A_x, \|v\|=1} \|J_f(x)\, v\| =: \|J_f(x)\|_{A_x}$$

This is the $A_x$-restricted operator norm of the Jacobian — the maximum output amplification of $f$ restricted to the admissible semantic directions. This is **Definition 4** in `theory/definitions.md`.

**The spectral gap idea:**

Consider the full spectral norm of the Jacobian: $\sigma_{\max}(J_f(x)) = \sup_{\|v\|=1} \|J_f(x)\, v\|$ (no restriction to $A_x$). This is the globally worst-case sensitivity direction.

The restricted norm $\|J_f(x)\|_{A_x}$ restricts to the semantic subspace. The gap between these two quantities:

$$\gamma(f, \mathcal{T}; x) := \sigma_{\max}(J_f(x)) - \|J_f(x)\|_{A_x}$$

measures how far the most sensitive directions are from the semantic manifold. If $\gamma$ is large, the model's most sensitive dimensions are geometrically orthogonal to $A_x$ — the model is sensitive to meaning-changing perturbations but invariant to meaning-preserving ones. That's exactly the desirable geometric property.

Normalized version: $\bar{\gamma}(f, \mathcal{T}; x) = 1 - \|J_f(x)\|_{A_x} / \sigma_{\max}(J_f(x)) \in [0, 1]$.

This became **Definition 5** (Semantic Spectral Gap).

**Implementation sketch for `jacobian.py`:**

```python
def directional_derivative_norm(
    fn: ForwardFn,
    x: torch.Tensor,    # (B, seq, hidden)
    v: torch.Tensor,    # (B, seq, hidden) — unit directions
) -> torch.Tensor:      # (B,)
    _, jvp_val = torch.autograd.functional.jvp(fn, x, v, create_graph=False)
    return jvp_val.flatten(start_dim=1).norm(dim=1)

def restricted_operator_norm(
    fn: ForwardFn,
    x: torch.Tensor,
    directions: torch.Tensor,    # (B, K, seq, hidden)
) -> torch.Tensor:               # (B,)
    norms = [directional_derivative_norm(fn, x, directions[:, k]) for k in range(K)]
    return torch.stack(norms, dim=1).max(dim=1).values
```

`torch.autograd.functional.jvp` computes the JVP in a single forward pass — O(1) backward cost regardless of input dimensionality. For a transformer with hidden_dim=768 and seq_len=128, the full Jacobian would be $128 \times 768 \times 768$-dimensional (impossible to materialize). JVP avoids this entirely.

### Results / Observations

Ran a quick test of the JVP-based restricted operator norm on BERT-base vs the finite-difference approximation from the sampling-based estimator. Want to check they agree in the small-$\varepsilon$ limit.

Setup: 20 examples from SST-2, 8 synonym directions each, $\varepsilon \in \{0.001, 0.01, 0.1\}$.

| $\varepsilon$ | Sampling-based Sens (mean) | JVP-based restricted norm (mean) | Ratio |
|---|---|---|---|
| 0.001 | 0.347 | 0.351 | 0.989 |
| 0.010 | 0.341 | 0.351 | 0.971 |
| 0.100 | 0.318 | 0.351 | 0.906 |

As $\varepsilon \to 0$, the sampling-based estimate converges to the JVP-based estimate. At $\varepsilon = 0.1$, there's a ~10% gap, which is the finite-$\varepsilon$ correction term. This is expected from the theory.

**Spectral gap on BERT-base (20 examples, 16 random probes for $\sigma_{\max}$):**

| Quantity | Mean | Std |
|---|---|---|
| Restricted norm $\|J_f\|_{A_x}$ | 0.351 | 0.089 |
| Full spectral norm $\sigma_{\max}$ | 0.847 | 0.142 |
| Normalized gap $\bar{\gamma}$ | 0.586 | 0.117 |

$\bar{\gamma} \approx 0.59$ for BERT-base on SST-2 — about 59% of the Jacobian's sensitivity budget is in non-semantic directions. Meaning: BERT is somewhat semantically structured, but there's meaningful sensitivity in arbitrary non-semantic directions too. This number needs comparison to other models to be interpretable.

### Lab Notes

The spectral gap idea is the most interesting thing to come out of today. No existing metric captures this. The geometric picture: the Jacobian at $x$ has a spectrum of singular values. The semantic manifold (spanned by $A_x$) is a subspace. The restricted norm projects the Jacobian onto this subspace. The gap tells you whether the top singular vectors are aligned with or orthogonal to the semantic subspace.

Intuitively, a good semantic model should have its top singular directions orthogonal to $A_x$ — meaning the most input-sensitive direction is NOT a semantic direction. The model should be most sensitive to meaning-changing perturbations, not meaning-preserving ones. That's $\bar{\gamma} = 1$.

The converse failure mode ($\bar{\gamma} = 0$) is interesting: the maximally sensitive direction IS a semantic direction. The model is most easily perturbed by meaning-preserving changes. This would be a model that has essentially no semantic invariance.

One thing I'm not sure about: whether $A_x$ as computed from synonym embedding differences is actually the correct semantic direction set. If the synonym directions don't span the true semantic manifold, the restricted norm is measuring something smaller than the actual semantic sensitivity. The gap could be artificially inflated.

Need to check: how does the spectral gap change as I add more synonym directions (increase $K$)? If $\bar{\gamma}$ decreases as $K$ increases (more directions captured → higher restricted norm), that suggests the current estimate is a lower bound.

### Hypotheses

1. **The restricted operator norm converges to the sampling-based sensitivity estimate as $\varepsilon \to 0$.** The theoretical limit should be exact. The gap at finite $\varepsilon$ is a second-order correction term — $O(\varepsilon)$ for smooth $f$.

2. **$\bar{\gamma}$ decreases as $K$ (number of synonym directions) increases.** Adding more semantic directions expands $A_x$ and can only increase $\|J_f\|_{A_x}$ (it's a sup over a larger set). So $\bar{\gamma}$ should be a decreasing function of $K$. The limit as $K \to \infty$ (if directions span the semantic manifold) is the "true" gap.

3. **RoBERTa will have a higher $\bar{\gamma}$ than BERT.** RoBERTa's pretraining (more data, longer training, dynamic masking) should produce more geometrically structured representations. Prediction: $\bar{\gamma}_{\text{RoBERTa}} > \bar{\gamma}_{\text{BERT}}$ by at least 0.05.
